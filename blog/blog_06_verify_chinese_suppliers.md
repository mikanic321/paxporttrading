## Introduction

Every year, thousands of international buyers lose money to unverified Chinese suppliers. Fake factories, bait-and-switch products, disappearing middlemen — these aren't rare horror stories. They happen every single day on platforms like Alibaba, Made-in-China, and Global Sources.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **finding a Chinese supplier is easy. Verifying one is hard.**

China's manufacturing ecosystem is massive — over 10 million registered manufacturers serving global markets. Within that ocean of suppliers, you'll find world-class factories producing for Apple, Nike, and Bosch right alongside fly-by-night operations running out of rented warehouses. The difference between a successful first order and a $50,000 disaster often comes down to one thing: **proper supplier due diligence**.

This guide gives you a practical, step-by-step framework to verify Chinese suppliers before you commit a single dollar. Whether you're [sourcing construction materials for Africa](/blog/africa-construction-materials-sourcing-guide), [importing machinery for South America](/blog/south-america-agricultural-machinery-sourcing), or buying consumer electronics for any market, these verification steps apply universally.

## Why Supplier Verification Matters

### The Cost of Getting It Wrong

Let's put numbers on the risk:

| Scenario | Typical Loss | Recovery Chance |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Fake supplier (takes payment, disappears) | $5,000–$50,000+ | Very Low (<5%) |
| Quality bait-and-switch (samples ≠ bulk) | $10,000–$100,000 | Low (10-20%) |
| Undisclosed middleman (inflated prices) | 15-40% markup per order | None (ongoing loss) |
| Non-compliant products (fails certification) | Full shipment value + penalties | Low |
| Factory closure mid-production | Deposit + delay costs | Very Low |

A single bad supplier can wipe out an entire season's profit margin. For businesses importing into markets with strict compliance requirements — like [Nigeria's SONCAP certification](/blog/nigeria-import-guide-soncap) or [Kenya's KEBS standards](/blog/how-to-import-from-china-to-kenya) — receiving non-compliant goods means the shipment can't even clear customs.

### Why Online Platforms Aren't Enough

Alibaba Gold Supplier status, "Verified" badges, and trade assurance programs provide a baseline — but they are **not** supplier verification. Here's why:

- **Gold Supplier** is a paid membership ($3,000–$6,000/year). Anyone can buy it.
- **Verified Supplier** confirms the company exists legally. It doesn't confirm they can produce your specific product at the quality you need.
- **Trade Assurance** covers payment disputes, not quality issues in most cases.
- **Reviews and ratings** can be manufactured or irrelevant to your product category.

Think of platform verification like a driver's license — it confirms someone is legally allowed to drive. It doesn't tell you if they're a good driver, if their car is reliable, or if they'll actually show up to pick you up.

**Real supplier verification goes deeper.** Much deeper.

## The 5-Step Supplier Verification Framework

### Step 1: Business License & Legal Entity Verification

**What to check:**
Every legitimate Chinese manufacturer has a business license (营业执照, *yíngyè zhízhào*). This is the single most important document to verify first.

**How to verify:**

1. **Request the business license directly.** Any legitimate supplier will provide it without hesitation. If they refuse or delay — that's your first red flag.

2. **Cross-reference on official government databases:**
   - **National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System** (国家企业信用信息公示系统): [gsxt.gov.cn](https://www.gsxt.gov.cn/) — Free, official, and covers all registered Chinese businesses
   - **Tianyancha** (天眼查): [tianyancha.com](https://www.tianyancha.com/) — More user-friendly, includes litigation history and related companies
   - **Qichacha** (企查查): [qcc.com](https://www.qcc.com/) — Similar to Tianyancha with additional financial data

3. **Key details to verify:**
   - **Company name** matches exactly (character-for-character in Chinese)
   - **Registration date** — How long have they been in business? Avoid companies less than 2 years old for large orders
   - **Registered capital** — While not always reflective of actual assets, extremely low registered capital (under ¥500,000) for a supposed "factory" is concerning
   - **Business scope** (经营范围) — Does it actually include manufacturing/production of your product? Many "factories" are actually trading companies
   - **Legal representative** — Note this name; it should match who signs contracts
   - **Operational status** — Check for any abnormal listings, penalties, or legal disputes

**Pro tip:** If the company claims to be a factory but their business scope says "trading" (贸易), "consulting" (咨询), or "technology services" (技术服务), they're almost certainly a middleman. That's not necessarily bad — but you should know what you're dealing with.

### Step 2: Factory Verification — Are They a Real Manufacturer?

**The trading company problem:**
An estimated 60-70% of "suppliers" on Alibaba are trading companies, not factories. Trading companies source from actual factories and add a markup (typically 10-30%). For some buyers, a good trading company adds value through quality control and logistics management. But if you're paying factory-direct prices, you should be buying from an actual factory.

**How to verify factory status:**

| Verification Method | Cost | Reliability | Time |
|---------------------|------|-------------|------|
| Video call factory tour | Free | Medium | 30 min |
| Request factory photos with your product name written on paper | Free | Medium | 1-2 days |
| Third-party factory audit (SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV) | $300–$800 | Very High | 1-2 weeks |
| Personal visit / agent visit | $500–$2,000 | Highest | 1-3 days |
| Google Maps / Baidu Maps satellite view | Free | Low-Medium | 10 min |

**Video call checklist:**
- Ask them to walk through the production floor (not just the showroom)
- Look for branded machinery, production lines in operation, worker activity
- Ask them to show a machine close-up and explain what it does
- Request they hold up a piece of paper with your company name and date
- Ask questions only a real manufacturer would know (production capacity per day, lead times for specific materials, minimum batch sizes for custom colors/specs)

**Satellite verification:**
Use Google Maps or Baidu Maps to check the factory address. A legitimate factory should show:
- A building complex consistent with manufacturing (not an apartment building or office tower)
- Loading docks or truck access
- Warehouse-scale structures

If the address points to a 15th-floor office in a commercial building, you're dealing with a trading company.

### Step 3: Product & Quality Verification

**Never skip samples.** Period.

The single most common mistake first-time importers make is skipping the sample stage to "save time." This shortcut has cost buyers millions in defective shipments.

**Sample verification process:**

1. **Request paid samples** ($50–$500 depending on product). Free samples often aren't representative of actual production quality — they're marketing materials.

2. **Order from multiple suppliers simultaneously.** Compare 3-5 suppliers side by side. The quality differences will be immediately obvious.

3. **Test samples against your specifications:**
   - Physical dimensions and weight
   - Material composition (request material test certificates / mill certificates)
   - Functional testing under realistic conditions
   - Compliance testing for target market standards (CE, SONCAP, KEBS, INMETRO, etc.)

4. **Check certifications:**
   - **ISO 9001** — Quality management system (basic but important)
   - **ISO 14001** — Environmental management
   - **Product-specific certifications** (CE for Europe, UL for USA, SONCAP for Nigeria)
   - Verify certificates directly with the issuing body — fake certificates are extremely common

5. **Third-party testing:**
   For orders above $10,000, consider sending samples to an independent testing lab (SGS, Intertek, Bureau Veritas). Cost: $200–$1,000 depending on tests. This is insurance, not expense.

**Key question to ask:** *"Can you provide test reports from an independent third-party lab for this product?"* If they only have self-issued "test reports," treat them with skepticism.

---

### Step 4: Financial & Operational Due Diligence

**Check financial stability:**
A supplier that goes bankrupt mid-production leaves you with no product and no refund. Basic financial checks include:

- **Years in business:** 5+ years indicates stability. Under 2 years = higher risk.
- **Number of employees:** Cross-reference their claimed employee count with social insurance records (available on Tianyancha). A "500-person factory" with 12 people on social insurance is lying.
- **Export history:** Ask for recent export records or customs declarations (with sensitive pricing redacted). Check their claimed export markets against reality.
- **Bank account verification:** The company name on the bank account should exactly match the company name on the business license. Payments to personal accounts or mismatched company names = extreme red flag.

**Operational checks:**

- **Production capacity:** Ask specific questions. "What's your monthly output for this product? How many production lines? What's the lead time for an order of [your quantity]?" Vague answers signal a trading company.
- **Client references:** Request 2-3 references from buyers in your region or industry. A reputable factory will have them. Call or email these references directly.
- **Payment terms:** Factories confident in their reputation will often accept 30/70 terms (30% deposit, 70% before shipment). Suppliers demanding 100% upfront for first orders are higher risk.

---

### Step 5: Pre-Shipment Inspection (PSI)

**The last line of defense.**

Even after verifying the supplier, you should inspect the actual production run before it ships. Pre-shipment inspection catches problems when you can still fix them — not after a container arrives 6,000 miles away.

**PSI options:**

| Inspection Type | When | Cost | Coverage |
|----------------|------|------|----------|
| During Production Inspection (DPI) | 20-40% of production complete | $250–$400/day | Catches problems early |
| Pre-Shipment Inspection (PSI) | 80-100% of production complete | $250–$400/day | Final quality check |
| Container Loading Inspection (CLI) | During container loading | $200–$350/day | Verifies quantity and packing |
| Full Inspection (DPI + PSI + CLI) | Multiple stages | $700–$1,200 total | Maximum protection |

**Major inspection companies in China:**
- SGS (Swiss — largest globally)
- Bureau Veritas (French)
- TÜV (German)
- Intertek (UK)
- Asia Inspection / QIMA (specialized for China)

**What inspectors check:**
- Product specifications match the approved sample
- AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) sampling — typically AQL 2.5 for major defects, AQL 4.0 for minor defects
- Packaging and labeling accuracy
- Quantity verification
- Functional testing on random samples
- Drop test, barcode scan test (where applicable)

**Cost perspective:** A PSI costs $250-$400. A defective container costs $10,000-$100,000+. The math is simple.

## Red Flags: How to Spot Supplier Scams

Experienced sourcing professionals develop a sixth sense for trouble. Here are the warning signs that should make you pause — or walk away entirely:

### 🚩 Immediate Deal-Breakers

| Red Flag | What It Means |
|----------|---------------|
| Refuses to provide business license | Likely not a legitimate company |
| Requests payment to a personal bank account | Possible scam or unregistered operation |
| Price is 30%+ below all other quotes | Bait-and-switch incoming, or inferior materials |
| Claims to manufacture everything (tiles AND electronics AND textiles) | Trading company pretending to be a factory |
| Pressures you to pay quickly ("price expires today!") | Classic high-pressure sales tactic |
| No physical factory address, or address doesn't match on maps | Shell company or virtual office |
| Can't do a video call showing the production floor | They don't have a production floor |

### 🟡 Yellow Flags (Investigate Further)

| Yellow Flag | What to Do |
|-------------|-----------|
| Company is less than 2 years old | Not disqualifying, but order smaller quantities first |
| Uses a Gmail/Yahoo/Hotmail email (not company domain) | Could be small but legitimate — verify business license |
| Offers unusually generous payment terms upfront | May be desperate for orders — check financial health |
| English is perfect but technical knowledge is weak | Likely a sales agent, not the factory — ask to speak with production |
| Won't provide client references | May be new to export — request additional verification |
| Price fluctuates significantly between conversations | Disorganized or testing your price sensitivity |

### 🟢 Good Signs

- Invites you to visit the factory
- Provides unsolicited documentation (certifications, test reports, client lists)
- Has consistent information across Alibaba, their website, and government records
- Asks detailed questions about your requirements before quoting
- Offers to arrange third-party inspection
- Has active social media showing factory operations (WeChat, Douyin/TikTok)
- Can speak knowledgeably about production processes, material sourcing, and lead times

## Factory Audit Checklist

Use this checklist when conducting or commissioning a factory audit in China. Print it, share it with your inspection agent, or send it to your sourcing partner.

### ✅ Company & Legal

- [ ] Business license obtained and verified on gsxt.gov.cn
- [ ] Company name, address, and legal representative match across all documents
- [ ] Business scope includes manufacturing of your product category
- [ ] No abnormal operating status, lawsuits, or penalties on record
- [ ] Registered capital is reasonable for claimed scale
- [ ] Company has been operating for 3+ years

### ✅ Factory & Production

- [ ] Factory address verified via satellite imagery
- [ ] Production floor inspected (in-person or via live video)
- [ ] Machinery is appropriate for the product being manufactured
- [ ] Production capacity matches claimed output
- [ ] Raw material storage area observed
- [ ] Worker count is consistent with claimed production capacity
- [ ] Quality control station/area exists on the production floor

### ✅ Quality & Certifications

- [ ] ISO 9001 certification verified with issuing body
- [ ] Product-specific certifications verified (CE, UL, SONCAP, etc.)
- [ ] Third-party test reports available for your product
- [ ] Sample quality matches or exceeds your requirements
- [ ] Material certificates / mill test certificates provided
- [ ] Defect rate data available from recent production runs

### ✅ Financial & Export Experience

- [ ] Bank account name matches business license name exactly
- [ ] Payment terms are reasonable (not 100% upfront)
- [ ] Export history verified (customs records or references)
- [ ] At least 2 client references contacted and confirmed
- [ ] No history of payment disputes or legal claims

### ✅ Communication & Professionalism

- [ ] Responds to inquiries within 24 hours
- [ ] Uses company domain email
- [ ] Provides detailed, specification-level answers to technical questions
- [ ] Willing to sign a purchase contract with quality terms and penalties
- [ ] Agrees to pre-shipment inspection by a third party
- [ ] Has a designated English-speaking contact for your account

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### How much does it cost to verify a Chinese supplier?

Basic verification (business license check, online research, video call) can be done for free or minimal cost. A professional third-party factory audit typically costs **$300–$800** per factory. Pre-shipment inspections run **$250–$400 per day**. For a comprehensive verification including factory visit, background check, and sample testing, budget **$1,000–$2,500** total. This is a fraction of what you'd lose to a bad supplier.

### Can I trust Alibaba Gold Suppliers?

Gold Supplier status means the company paid for a premium membership — it's not a quality guarantee. Many excellent suppliers are Gold Suppliers, but so are some unreliable ones. Use platform verification as a starting point, not a conclusion. Always conduct independent verification using the steps in this guide.

### Should I visit the factory in person?

For orders above $50,000 or for establishing a long-term supply relationship, an in-person visit is highly recommended. You'll learn more in a 4-hour factory walk-through than in months of email exchanges. If you can't visit personally, hire a sourcing agent (like Paxport) to visit on your behalf and provide a detailed report with photos and video.

### How do I verify if a supplier is a factory or trading company?

Check their business license — the business scope (经营范围) will indicate whether they're registered for manufacturing (生产/制造) or trading (贸易/销售). Also check their address on satellite maps, ask detailed production questions, and request a live video tour of the factory floor. Trading companies will struggle with all of these.

### What's the difference between a factory audit and a pre-shipment inspection?

A **factory audit** evaluates the supplier's overall capability — facilities, equipment, workforce, quality systems, and management. It answers the question: *"Can this factory produce my product reliably?"* A **pre-shipment inspection (PSI)** checks a specific production run against your order specifications. It answers: *"Did they actually produce what I ordered?"* Both are important. The factory audit happens before you place the order; the PSI happens before you pay the balance and ship.

### How long does the full verification process take?

A thorough supplier verification typically takes **2-4 weeks**, including:
- Business license verification: 1-2 days
- Online research and background checks: 2-3 days
- Sample ordering and evaluation: 1-2 weeks
- Factory audit (if commissioned): 1-2 weeks
- Reference checks: 3-5 days

Some steps can run in parallel. If you're working with a sourcing agent like Paxport who has existing factory relationships, the timeline can be compressed to **1-2 weeks**.

### Is it safe to pay Chinese suppliers by T/T (wire transfer)?

T/T (telegraphic transfer) is the most common payment method and is generally safe **if** you verify the supplier first and structure payments correctly. Best practice: pay **30% deposit** to start production, and **70% balance** after pre-shipment inspection approval. Never pay 100% upfront to a new supplier. For added protection on first orders, consider using a letter of credit (L/C) or Alibaba Trade Assurance.

### What should I do if I discover a supplier is fraudulent?

1. **Stop all payments immediately.** Contact your bank to attempt a recall if payment was recent (within 24-48 hours).
2. **Document everything.** Save all communications, contracts, payment receipts, and evidence.
3. **Report to the platform** (Alibaba, Made-in-China, etc.) if you found them there.
4. **File a police report** in China through the local Public Security Bureau (PSB) — a local agent can assist with this.
5. **Consult a lawyer** specializing in China trade law if significant amounts are involved.
6. **Share your experience** on import forums (Import Yeti, Reddit r/importing) to warn others.

Recovery rates for international trade fraud are low. Prevention through proper verification is always more effective than recovery after the fact.
